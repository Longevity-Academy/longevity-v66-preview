Biomarkers & Testing

Measuring your health is not the same as managing it — but it's a necessary starting point. These three articles cover which biomarkers actually matter for longevity, how to interpret them without clinical training, and how to use your own data to make better decisions over time.

Article 01

The Blood Tests Your Annual Physical Probably Misses

A standard annual physical typically includes a basic metabolic panel, complete blood count, and sometimes a lipid panel. These are useful, but they cover a narrow slice of the biomarkers most relevant to long-term health and aging. A more complete longevity-oriented panel requires some additions — most of which are inexpensive and available through standard labs when requested.

The goal of a broader panel is not to generate anxiety about marginal findings. It's to establish a personal baseline that allows you to track trends over years, identify early metabolic changes before they become pathology, and close the gap between "normal by lab reference range" and "optimal for your long-term health." Those two categories are not always the same thing.

What to Add to Your Standard Panel

Fasting insulin. As discussed elsewhere in this series, fasting insulin reveals early insulin resistance before glucose rises. A result above 7–10 mIU/L in a fasting state, combined with normal fasting glucose, is a clinically meaningful signal that warrants attention. This is inexpensive to add and rarely included in standard panels without specifically requesting it.

HbA1c. Glycated hemoglobin reflects average blood glucose over the prior three months. It is more sensitive than a single fasting glucose for detecting metabolic trends and is routinely used in diabetes management. Its inclusion in preventive panels is warranted for anyone over 40 or with metabolic risk factors.

hsCRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein). CRP is an acute-phase protein released in response to inflammation. The high-sensitivity version of the test detects low-grade systemic inflammation that doesn't appear in standard CRP tests. Elevated hsCRP is an independent cardiovascular risk factor and a useful proxy for chronic inflammatory load. A level below 1 mg/L is associated with lower cardiovascular risk; above 3 mg/L warrants further evaluation.

Apolipoprotein B (ApoB). Standard lipid panels measure LDL cholesterol as a proxy for cardiovascular risk, but LDL-C has significant limitations. ApoB directly counts the number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles, which is a more accurate predictor of cardiovascular risk. Two people with identical LDL-C can have significantly different ApoB — and the one with higher particle count has higher risk. Many cardiologists consider ApoB the single most informative cardiovascular risk marker available in routine bloodwork.

Vitamin D (25-OH). Deficiency is common and affects immune function, bone health, mood, and metabolic processes. Knowing your level allows targeted supplementation rather than guessing.

Making Sense of Results

Lab reference ranges are designed to capture the normal range in the tested population, not to define optimal health. A fasting insulin of 9 mIU/L is "normal" by most lab ranges; it is also twice what many metabolic health clinicians consider a target. Working with a clinician who understands longevity-oriented interpretation, or educating yourself on evidence-based optimal ranges, produces more actionable insights from your results.

Article 02

Heart Rate Variability: What It Measures and What It Actually Tells You

Heart rate variability (HRV) has become one of the most discussed metrics in consumer health technology — tracked by Garmin, Oura, Whoop, and Apple Watch, displayed as a dashboard number, and frequently the subject of concern when it drops. But many people who track their HRV have an imprecise sense of what they're measuring and what it means for their health.

HRV is a measure of the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. A heart rate of 60 beats per minute doesn't mean one beat every exactly 1,000 milliseconds — the intervals vary slightly from beat to beat. Greater variability generally indicates a well-functioning autonomic nervous system that's responsive to changing physiological demands. Lower variability — more clock-like regularity — often indicates a system that's under stress, recovering from illness, or operating at reduced adaptive capacity.

Why HRV Reflects More Than Just Recovery

The autonomic nervous system — which HRV reflects — coordinates the activity of organs throughout the body in response to internal and external demands. Parasympathetic activity (the "rest and digest" branch) increases beat-to-beat variability; sympathetic activity (the "fight or flight" branch) reduces it. A high HRV reading indicates relatively strong parasympathetic tone, which is associated with good stress recovery, healthy cardiovascular function, and robust immune and metabolic regulation.

Research has linked low HRV to higher cardiovascular mortality, faster cognitive aging, worse glycemic control, and poorer sleep quality. These associations are present across diverse populations and persist in analyses that control for age, fitness level, and other confounders. HRV is, in effect, a window into the autonomic regulation that underlies many physiological processes relevant to aging.

How to Use HRV Data Usefully

The most important principle in HRV interpretation is that your own baseline is the reference point, not any population average. Consumer device HRV numbers vary by measurement method, device, and timing — comparing your 45 to someone else's 62 is not meaningful. Tracking your own trend over weeks and months, and identifying which lifestyle factors (sleep quality, alcohol, intense training, illness, stress) correlate with rises and falls in your personal baseline, is where the actionable information lives.

Behaviors that consistently improve HRV over time include regular aerobic exercise (particularly zone 2 training), adequate sleep, stress management practices, and limiting alcohol. HRV responds quickly to alcohol — even one to two drinks suppresses HRV measurably the following night — which makes it one of the more immediate feedback tools for assessing recovery-relevant behaviors. Used as a trend indicator rather than a daily report card, HRV is among the more genuinely informative metrics available in consumer health monitoring.

Article 03

Interpreting Your Own Health Data: A Framework for Making Sense of Numbers

Access to health data has expanded dramatically. People can now track glucose, sleep stages, heart rate variability, oxygen saturation, skin temperature, and dozens of other variables with consumer devices that fit on a wrist or arm. The challenge has shifted from access to interpretation: how do you make sense of a constant stream of numbers without clinical training, without developing anxiety about normal variation, and without making decisions on insufficient evidence?

A few principles help. First, a single data point is almost never meaningful in isolation. A fasting glucose of 105 on one morning could reflect a poor night's sleep, a stress response, or a larger carbohydrate meal the night before — or it could reflect nothing in particular. One number creates no pattern. Trends over weeks to months are where interpretable information lives.

The Signal vs. Noise Problem

Consumer health devices produce enormous amounts of data, most of which is noise. Wearable sleep stages, for instance, have poor agreement with polysomnography (the clinical gold standard for sleep measurement). Wrist-based heart rate measurements are accurate at rest but less reliable at higher intensities. Interstitial glucose from CGMs lags blood glucose by 10-15 minutes and can be affected by medications and alcohol.

Understanding the limitations of each device doesn't mean discarding the data — it means calibrating confidence appropriately. Use CGM data to identify patterns across meals and behaviors, not to make decisions based on any individual reading. Use HRV to track weekly averages, not to interpret single-night values. Use step counts and activity data to assess general activity trends over weeks, not to evaluate individual days.

The question worth asking of any health metric is: does tracking this produce decisions I wouldn't otherwise make? If it does — if seeing your post-meal glucose motivates you to take a walk you'd otherwise skip, or if HRV trends help you calibrate training intensity — it's adding value. If it produces daily anxiety without changing behavior, it may be generating more cost than benefit.

Building a Personal Health Baseline

The most useful function of health data is establishing a personal baseline from which to measure change. This requires consistency: measuring under consistent conditions (same time of day, same pre-conditions for fasting tests), using the same device for the same metric across time, and tracking long enough to see meaningful trends — typically months to years rather than days to weeks.

Annual blood panels, taken at the same lab, under the same conditions, with the same panel of tests, provide a time-series that becomes genuinely informative over years. An HbA1c trending from 5.4 to 5.6 to 5.8 over three years — still "normal" at each measurement — tells a different story than a stable 5.3 across the same period. That trend visibility is what makes longitudinal personal data more valuable than any single measurement, however comprehensive.
